# Sensory Reference for Yerba Mate

## Introduction

This project began with the goal of gathering and organizing empirical observations about sensory experiences with yerba mate, drawing on analogies and methodologies commonly used in coffee, tea, and beer tasting, and making them accessible in a public and structured way. By being shared openly, the material aims to encourage new explorations, broaden the exchange of experiences, and contribute to the collective building of knowledge on the topic.

Nothing here seeks to replace the experience of a sommelier or specialist. The point is to document observations, organize vocabulary, and value this repertoire openly while building collective knowledge. The idea is to make sensory discussion around yerba mate a bit easier and more precise, without treating yerba mate only through the lens of "good," "bad," or "different." You can contribute improvements, new notes, or corrections by [email](mailto:mate@rodgz.com).

To make the reading easier, the guide moves from foundation to application:

1. principles and typicity references
2. sensory dimensions, structure, and lexicon
3. preparation variables and defects
4. evaluation protocols and a condensed score sheet

## 1. Assessment Principles

To make readings more consistent and comparable, this guide relies on four principles:

1. In more established base styles, style usually comes before score.
   An attribute that is desirable in one style may register as noise in another. Fresh chimarrão can present vivid green notes, umami, and body; aged mate accepts more hay, wood, and dried fruit. In more authorial or hybrid proposals, that filter can share space with originality, integration, and the blend's intended profile.
2. Intensity is not quality.
   High bitterness, strong smoke, or heavy body do not mean excellence by themselves. What matters is integration with the style.
3. A descriptor is not a defect.
   Earthy, resinous, woody, balsamic, and smoky notes can be positive when they are clean and coherent with the style.
4. Preparation changes the reading.
   Water, temperature, grind, the proportion of powder and stems, the vessel, and the extraction stage radically change perception.

## 2. Typicity References

### 2.1 Base Styles

| Base style | Raw material and process | Visual signature | Expected sensory axis | Risks of misreading |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Traditional Brazilian chimarrão | Very fresh yerba, fine grind, high powder content, little to no aging | Intense green, vivid brightness, fine texture | Fresh vegetal, chlorophyll, vegetable broth, umami, dense body, cane sweetness | Mistaking green bitterness for a defect when it is structural; mistaking umami for an excessive "marine" note |
| Argentine mate con palo | Coarser leaf, meaningful presence of stems, extended aging | Yellow-green to olive straw, more open cut | Hay, dried leaf, light wood, cereal, clean earthiness, rounded bitterness | Judging as "lifeless" something that is simply more mature and less green |
| Uruguayan despalado or PU-1 mate | Fine cut, almost no stems, relevant powder content, usually aged | Olive green to yellow, compact appearance | High structural bitterness, full body, dark earthiness, bitter cocoa, dried fruit, integrated smoke when barbacuá is present | Reading intensity as roughness; overextracting and creating metallic or ashy notes |
| Tereré | Coarser cut, higher proportion of stems, cold extraction | Bright green to straw green | Refreshing herbal notes, fresh cucumber, mint, sap, low perception of bitterness | Taking the lack of body as a defect when the point is freshness and lightness |
| Toasted mate | More intense heat treatment, Maillard profile | Brown to dark chestnut | Roasted barley, toast, burnt sugar, cocoa, dark caramel | Excess roasting creates charcoal, ash, and aggressive dryness |

### 2.2 Cross-Cutting Compositional Categories

Pure yerba blends and compound mates are not isolated base styles. In many cases they relate to a base style (chimarrão, Argentine, Uruguayan, tereré, or toasted), but they can shift part of the reading toward the sensory direction of the final composition. For that reason, it helps to judge references to origin, compositional integration, and coherence of the final result at the same time.

| Category | What it is | How to read it sensorially | Risk of misreading |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Pure Yerba Mate Blend** (mix of harvests, origins, or processes) | Only yerba mate, combining different lots (e.g. shade-grown leaf with sun-grown leaf, young yerba with aged yerba, standard drying with a touch of barbacuá). | In a standard reading, the focus is usually on **balance and consistency**, rounding off peaks: the bitterness of a young lot is tamed by the sweetness and wood of an aged lot. In more authorial proposals, the blend may instead pursue contrast or a signature profile, as long as the result stays coherent. | Judging the yerba as "lacking personality" or "ordinary" because it has no extreme peaks, when the blend may aim either for a round, harmonious drink or, in authorial cases, for intentional contrast. |
| **Compound Mate** (botanical blends / *compuestas*) | Base yerba mate mixed with botanicals (herbs, flowers, spices) or flavorings. | In a standard reading, the added botanical leads the aroma (citrus, mentholated, floral), while the base yerba mate usually provides structure, rounded bitterness, and background body. Synergy remains the key point, but more authorial proposals may seek deliberate contrast rather than seamless fusion alone. | Letting the flavoring "blind" the analysis to defects in the base yerba (e.g. using too much mint to hide mold); penalizing the sample for "not tasting much like mate" without considering whether the sensory proposal intentionally prioritizes contrast, freshness, or another direction. |

**On processes:**
Here, `barbacuá` is treated as a process marker rather than an isolated style. When well executed, it adds sweet wood smoke, resin, and depth; when poorly executed, it leads to acridity, ash, and aggressive phenols.

## 3. Evaluation Model

### 3.1 Dimensions of Analysis

| Dimension | What to observe | Recommended language |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Appearance | color, uniformity, presence of powder, leaves, and stems, signs of aging or oxidation | vivid green, olive green, straw, chestnut, fine cut, open cut |
| Dry aroma | volatiles before hydration | grass, hay, cedar, cereal, mint, smoke, dried fruit |
| Wet aroma | evolution after contact with water | vegetable broth, damp earth, wood, raw cane sugar, citrus peel, cocoa |
| Basic tastes | the physiological response of the taste buds | residual sweetness, bitterness, umami, subtle acidity, light salinity |
| Flavor (retronasal) | aromas perceived in the mouth that rise into the nasal cavity | vegetal, earthy, woody, toasted, balsamic, smoky |
| Intensity | overall strength of the perceived stimuli | delicate, moderate, intense, expansive |
| Structure | astringency, body, texture, oiliness, juiciness | fine astringency, aggressive astringency, syrupy body, light body, silky texture, subtle oiliness |
| Persistence | duration of the aftertaste and sensations after the sip | short, medium, long, very long |
| Finish | direction and quality of the aftertaste as it resolves | clean finish, sweet finish, dry finish, mineral finish, smoky finish |
| Typicity | adherence to the proposed style or coherence with the intended sensory direction | exemplary, correct, diffuse, stripped of character |
| Cleanliness | absence of foreign notes or defects | clean, lightly contaminated, oxidized, moldy |

### 3.2 Sensory and Perceptual Domains

The same yerba can activate different perceptual layers. Separating them helps prevent aromatic descriptors, basic tastes, and physical sensations from being treated as if they were the same thing.

| Domain | What it is | Examples | How to use it in the text |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Taste | direct response of the taste buds | sweet, bitter, umami, subtle acidity, light salinity | use it to record basic tastes, not aromatic families |
| Aroma | volatiles perceived in the nose, before or outside the sip | hay, cedar, grass, smoke, chamomile | appears mainly in the dry aroma and the wet aroma |
| Flavor (retronasal) | aromatic perception that rises from the mouth to the nasal cavity | vegetal, earthy, toasted, balsamic, dried fruit | this is the main focus of the expanded sensory lexicon |
| Trigeminal | physical sensation of freshness, pungency, anesthesia, or cooling | minty freshness, camphor, tingling, pungency | describe it separately from aroma and taste |
| Structure | the beverage's tactile and architectural behavior | body, texture, astringency, juiciness, persistence | evaluate it together with the style and the preparation |

When mint, camphor, or minerality appear in the text, it is worth distinguishing whether the reference is aromatic or physical. The aroma may recall mint; the trigeminal sensation may cool like mint. Physiologically, mint, camphor, and related cues can suggest freshness without any real drop in temperature. This is closer to activation of trigeminal receptors such as `TRPM8` than to a "cold aroma." In practice, it helps to separate minty smell from the physical sensation of cooling.

### 3.3 Mouthfeel, Trigeminality, and Structure

Mate stands apart from many teas by combining bitterness, drying sensation, colloidal density, and persistence at high levels.

This section deals with tactile, physical, and architectural sensations. Although bitterness is physiologically a basic taste, it is included here because it acts as a structural axis in yerba mate, supporting body, progression, and persistence in the cup. Whenever freshness, warming, tingling, or slight anesthesia are present, the reading enters the trigeminal field, and it helps not to confuse it with aroma.

| Attribute | How to describe it | Interpretation |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Bitterness | low, medium, high; green, sharp, rounded, deep | the more balanced bitterness supports the profile without scraping |
| Astringency | drying, tactile, gingival, short, prolonged | some dryness is normal; coarse, hollow roughness tends to indicate defect or overextraction |
| Pungency | tingling, slight numbness, spicy | a slightly anesthetic physical sensation (like clove), common at the back of the mouth |
| Intensity | low, medium, high; contained, expansive, growing | measures the beverage's overall impact without confusing force with quality |
| Body | light, medium, full, syrupy, dense | chimarrão and fine-cut mates tend toward more body; tereré tends toward less |
| Texture | silky, floury, creamy, sandy | fine powder can create silkiness; poor extraction can seem rough |
| Juiciness | salivating, brothy, watery | umami and freshness often increase juiciness |
| Sensory persistence | how long flavor and sensation remain after the sip | high persistence does not imply quality |
| Finish | how the profile closes in the aftertaste | a good finish can be short or long, as long as it is coherent and clean |

In practical terms:

- Persistence: how long it lasts.
- Finish: how it ends.

Two important contrasts:

- Green bitterness: recalls chlorophyll, crushed leaf, and a sharp point.
- Mature bitterness: recalls cocoa, nut, and a darker, more persistent infusion.

## 4. Expanded Sensory Lexicon

Not every descriptor fits every yerba. The proper use of the lexicon depends on style, process, and preparation. The categories listed below (from 4.1 to 4.9) deal with aromatic and retronasal evaluation, that is, flavor through the nasal route, rather than with basic tastes perceived directly on the tongue's taste buds.

### 4.1 Vegetal and Green

The central family of mate, especially in fresher yerbas with higher powder content.

- Fresh green: freshly cut grass, torn leaf, snow pea, raw spinach, chlorophyll
- Wet green: sap, macerated leaf, leaf broth, pureed leafy greens
- Cooked green: cooked spinach, warmed green bean, wilted leafy greens
- Dry green: pale hay, clean straw, dried green tea leaf

As a cross-reference, the fresher pole recalls sencha, culinary matcha, and green peas; the drier pole approaches oxidized green tea or sweet hay.

In practice, there is a gray zone between vegetal and herbal. In general, vegetal points more toward leaf, chlorophyll, sap, and green broth; herbal points more toward terpenes, balsamic lift, and refreshment.

### 4.2 Umami, Brothy, and Marine/Saline Notes

Most noticeable in very fresh, full-bodied chimarrão-style yerbas with plenty of fine powder, as well as in more robust Uruguayan PU-1 profiles.

- kombu
- restrained nori
- vegetable broth
- vegetable cooking water
- subtle white mushroom
- subtle minerality in the aftertaste
- ferrous touch / tactile minerality
- subtle salinity

In practical reading, this family can be positive when it appears as aromatic depth and a brothy impression, often accompanying perceptions of umami and increased salivation. Light minerality in PU-1 can contrast well with bitterness. However, if it comes across as excessive algae, sludge, or an oxidized ferrous note, the reading leaves the field of quality and enters the realm of extraction or contamination defect.

### 4.3 Earthy, Mineral, and Forest Floor

More common in aged mates and more mature profiles.

- damp earth
- petrichor
- clay
- fresh root
- clean moss
- autumn leaf
- wet stone

When clean, earthiness suggests maturity and complexity; when it feels closed, moldy, or excessively dusty, it points toward defect.

### 4.4 Wood, Resin, and Bark

Important in Argentine and Uruguayan profiles, as well as in products with more pronounced drying.

- cedar
- tree bark
- clean sawdust
- subtle pine
- resin
- clean cigar box

As calibration, good wood recalls dry cedar and clean fiber; bad wood recalls damp woodworking dust or cardboard.

### 4.5 Toasted, Cereal, and Maillard

The dominant family in toasted mate and a secondary family in some mature mates.

- toast
- roasted barley
- baked cereal
- bread crust
- cacao nibs
- dark caramel
- burnt sugar

A well-resolved toasted mate is closer to cereal, bread, and cocoa than to charcoal. It tends to recall clean roasted malt more than an ashtray.

### 4.6 Fruity, Sweet, and Floral

In mate, this family tends to be subtler and usually appears as nuance rather than protagonist.

- sugarcane
- raw cane sugar
- light honey
- prune
- raisin
- dried fig
- orange peel
- dried flower
- chamomile


### 4.7 Herbal, Balsamic, and Refreshing

Very relevant in tereré and in yerbas with a high-toned, bright profile.

- mint
- peppermint
- fresh cucumber
- eucalyptus
- fennel
- light anise
- restrained camphor
- lemongrass

Well-integrated herbal and balsamic notes open up the profile; excess can seem medicinal. When accompanied by a physical cooling sensation, that reading is best recorded separately under the trigeminal axis.

### 4.8 Spices and Seasonings

Common in more mature, toasted, or more heavily dried mates.

- clove
- subtle cinnamon
- dried ginger
- nutmeg
- light pepper notes

A good spicy note complements the woody and toasted profile, adding complexity and depth in the mouth.

### 4.9 Smoky

A high-impact descriptor that is easy to exaggerate. It is worth separating it carefully from dirty notes such as "ash."

- sweet wood smoke
- cold embers
- toasted wood
- dry tobacco
- cured leaf
- clean fireplace

As sensory calibration, when smoke is well integrated, it tends to recall wood and transparent smoke, not soot or excessive phenols. In a good reading, it comes across as clean and culinary, unlike the defect of "ash," which brings dryness and the taste of burnt powder.

## 5. Variables That Change the Sensory Reading

Before concluding that a yerba is good or bad, review the preparation.

### 5.1 Water (TDS and pH) (WIP)

- **pH:** *(WIP — reference ranges and sensory influences to be verified.)*
- **Temperature:** Boiling water is rarely a good reference standard for green mate, because it speeds up pigment degradation and increases the volatilization of more delicate aromatic compounds. Higher temperatures also encourage the extraction of compounds associated with bitterness and dryness, especially tannins, but also other polyphenols, saponins, caffeoylquinic acids, and caffeine, making the beverage more bitter, more astringent, and less aromatic.

### 5.2 Reference Temperature

- Chimarrão: `60` to `75` degrees C
- Hot aged mate: `60` to `75` degrees C
- Tereré: `5` to `10` degrees C, depending on the proposed approach
- Toasted mate as an infusion: `85` to `92` degrees C

### 5.3 Grind and Powder Proportion

- More powder: more body, more umami, more extraction, more risk of harshness
- More stems: more lightness, greater permeability in the gourd, a more open aromatic profile, and lower density
- Finer cut: greater initial intensity and greater sensitivity to temperature error

### 5.4 Extraction Stage

- First sips: peak impact, bitterness, and texture
- Mid-session: greater balance and clearer reading of complexity
- End of the session: body drops, with a relative increase in hay, minerality, and sweet residual notes

Do not judge a yerba only by the first sip, nor only once it is washed out.

## 6. Defects and Deviations

| Defect | How it shows up | Note |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Mold or mildew | closed cabinet, damp cloth, cellar | serious defect |
| Oxidation or cardboard | old paper, stale cereal, tired fat | reduces brightness and cleanliness |
| Rancidity | old oil, aged nut | more common in poorly stored product |
| Lactic / Sweaty | sour yogurt, cheese, sweat, damp cloth | unwanted lactic fermentation caused by delayed drying or delays before the initial heat-treatment stage. Different from vinegary/acetic notes. |
| Vinegary / Acetic | vinegar, volatile sourness, vinegared fruit | unwanted acetic fermentation or acidification; clear defect. |
| Acrylic smoke | burnt plastic, rubber, aggressive soot | different from clean barbacuá |
| Ash | ashtray, burnt powder, dry charcoal dust | unlike clean smoke, it dries the mouth |
| Burnt or charred | charcoal, hollow bitterness | masks the rest of the profile |
| Metallic | coin, iron, metallic bitterness | it may arise from very soft water, excessive temperature, or aggressive extraction, not only from the yerba itself |
| Dirty dust | stale powder, shelf, damp bag | do not confuse with clean earthiness |

## 7. Session Tasting (Vessel/Utensil)

The protocol below prioritizes repeatability. For comparable sensory analysis, the session should preferably be carried out in an inert vessel such as glass, stainless steel, or porcelain. Calabash gourds, wood, and other materials with their own sensory signature can still be used, but they are best treated as contextual readings and their influence on the result should be recorded.

When comparing samples, it helps to work in randomized or blind order whenever possible, with neutral water for rinsing and short pauses between tastings.

1. Identify the expected style or the intent of the infusion.
   Record origin, cut, presence of stems, and whether it is identified as fresh, aged, barbacuá, toasted, or as some hybrid approach.
2. Perform the visual inspection.
   Observe color, liveliness, homogeneity, and the proportion of powder, leaves, and stems.
3. Evaluate the dry aroma.
   Look for the dominant family and note two to four objective descriptors.
4. Hydrate at a temperature compatible with the style or intended profile.
   Use the ranges in section 5.2 as a starting point. Do not use boiling water as the reference standard for green mate.
5. Evaluate the wet aroma before the first sip.
   Damp earth, wood, vegetable broth, and residual sweetness often appear here.
6. Taste in cycles.
   Separate the reading into the beginning, middle, and end of the session. In comparative sessions, try to record at least `8` to `10` cycles or until washout becomes clearly perceptible.
   In a gourd, the mechanics of the bombilla itself can draw air along with the liquid and amplify retronasal perception, in an effect functionally close to the *slurp* used in cupping.
7. Describe intensity, structure, and finish.
   Record overall intensity, bitterness, astringency, body, texture, juiciness, persistence, and finish.
8. Judge typicity and cleanliness.
   Ask whether the yerba fulfills the proposed style or the stated sensory intent, and whether there are clear defects.
9. Only then assign a score.
   A score without description is of little use; description without context is not enough either.

Whenever possible, do at least one round without a visible label. Even partial blinding already reduces brand, origin, and expectation bias.

## 8. Complementary Objective Evaluation Protocols (Inert Tasting)

When the goal is to evaluate the raw material naked and on its own, it helps to work with an inert vessel and isolate the yerba from the variables introduced by calabash, wood, ceramic, or other materials with their own sensory signature. The methods below work as complementary tests to the session protocol: they help isolate aromatic defects, compare yield, and observe the yerba's physical behavior without the interpretive weight of the vessel. Two methods can be used: the **Cycle Test (Dynamic)**, which tests the physical behavior and yield of the yerba, and the **Infusion Test**, focused on detecting aromatic defects.

### 8.1 Cycle Test in Glass/Stainless Steel (Behavior and Yield)
A very useful method for evaluating the physical structure of the powder, the development of bitterness, and the yerba's washout limit.

*   **Equipment:** Smooth transparent cup (thick glass, approx. 200 ml) and a simple stainless steel bombilla.
*   **Temperature:** 70°C to 75°C (practical range for standardization).
*   **Procedure:**
    1. Fill 2/3 of the cup with the yerba. Visually inspect the cut and the dry aroma against the light.
    2. Perform a standardized initial hydration: moisten the base with warm water and wait for 1 minute.
    3. **Olfactory diagnosis:** Smell inside the cup immediately after hydration. This is the moment when heavy volatiles from unwanted fermentation or mold are easiest to detect.
    4. Start the cycles with water at 70°C.
*   **What to judge:**
    *   *Attack (Cycles 1 to 3):* Intensity of green or mature bitterness; presence of acute defects (ash, acrylic smoke).
    *   *Body (Cycles 4 to 8):* Integration of umami, resin, wood, and sweetness. Texture and juiciness.
    *   *Yield (Cycles 10+):* Speed of flavor drop-off (washout). Is the finish clean and sweet, or does it become thin, rough, and metallic?

For comparable readings, it helps not to stop before at least `10` cycles unless a severe defect makes the session unusable.

### 8.2 French Infusion Test (Isolation of Defects and Extracts)
This is an attempted adaptation from specialty tea and coffee tasting. The method removes the colloidal body (the "creaminess" of chimarrão) and forces an aggressive extraction to lay bare the lot's aromatic qualities or hidden defects. Because mate contains a lot of powder, paper filters steal essential oils and tea-style methods clog. The practical solution is the steel press.

Here, `80°C` is not meant to be delicate. It pushes extraction to expose tannins, other polyphenols, caffeine, astringency, and latent deviations in the lot, in a logic close to forced tasting used in tea cupping.

*   **Equipment:** Small glass French press and a white porcelain bowl.
*   **Parameters:** `5g` to `7g` of yerba for `150ml` of water at `80°C`.
*   **Procedure:**
    1. Place the yerba at the bottom of the French press.
    2. Pour the 80°C water vigorously so it swirls and wets all the powder. Start the timer for **4 minutes**.
    3. At 2 minutes, open and gently break the crust with a spoon, bringing your nose close to evaluate the wet vapor (forest, tobacco, earthy notes).
    4. At 4 minutes, lower the plunger *very slowly* so as not to stir up the fine powder at the bottom.
    5. Serve the liquor (the green-gold or chestnut-colored liquid) into the white porcelain bowl.
*   **What to judge:**
    *   Without the physical impact of powder on the tongue, the evaluator can focus 100% on **acidity, residual sweetness, and aftertaste (flavor)**. Storage defects (cardboard, rancidity) become undeniable as the beverage begins to cool in the white porcelain.

## 9. Condensed Evaluation Sheet

A simple scale helps compare samples without making the analysis rigid.

| Criterion | Range | What to consider |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Typicity | 0 to 5 | how faithful the sample is to the style or the stated intent |
| Cleanliness | 0 to 5 | absence of mold, oxidation, bad smoke, fermentation |
| Complexity | 0 to 5 | quantity and articulation of sensory families |
| Balance | 0 to 5 | integration among aroma, flavor, body, and finish |
| Structure | 0 to 5 | quality of astringency, body, texture, and persistence |
| Overall pleasure | 0 to 5 | final synthesis of the whole |

Intensity and persistence can be recorded in complementary fields, and perceived physiological effect can be noted separately, without automatically changing the main score.

| Complementary field | Suggested record |
| --- | --- |
| Evaluated style / declared proposal | free text |
| Intensity | 0 to 5 |
| Persistence | 0 to 5 |
| Perceived physiological effect (optional) | according to section 9.2 |

### 9.2 Suggested Scoring Anchors

To increase repeatability, use `5`, `3`, `1`, and `0` as anchors, and treat `4` and `2` as intermediate points between them.

| Criterion | 5 | 3 | 1 | 0 |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Typicity | exemplary within the style or intended profile | recognizable, but diffuse | outside the expected style or misaligned with the stated intent | impossible to classify safely |
| Cleanliness | no perceptible deviations | small noises, without compromising the reading | clear defects already interfere with judgment | dominant defect or compromised sample |
| Complexity | several integrated and legible families | some perceptible families, without great depth | short or monotonous profile | no useful complexity for analysis |
| Balance | high integration between attack, body, and finish | functional whole, but with rough edges | disconnected or unbalanced parts | broken or incoherent whole |
| Structure | body, astringency, texture, and persistence support the beverage well | correct but ordinary structure | weak, rough, or hollow structure | structure unfit for reliable reading |
| Overall pleasure | memorable and repeatable beverage | pleasant, with reservations | not very pleasurable | clear rejection |

In practical reading, `5` and `4` indicate strong performance within the style or stated intent; `3` indicates a correct sample, but without great distinction; `2` and `1` indicate relevant limitations; `0` can be reserved for unworkable, defective, or out-of-frame samples.

The ranges below are interpretive rather than symmetrical. The lower zone is intentionally broader so it can group compromised, defective, or out-of-frame samples.

- `26` to `30`: exemplary within the style or highly coherent with the intended profile
- `21` to `25`: very good, with minor limitations
- `16` to `20`: correct, but without great distinction
- `10` to `15`: below expected or poorly executed
- below `10`: compromised by defects or loss of character

In collaborative use, it helps to align these anchors with one or two reference samples before the session. That reduces scale drift between tasters.

### 9.2 Perceived Physiological Effect (Optional)

This dimension does not assess sensory quality; it only records the subjective perception of the session's effect. Because it belongs more to the functional and physiological field than to the sensory one, it is best kept in this complementary part of the sheet, separate from aroma, flavor, structure, and technical scoring.

| Attribute | How to describe it |
| --- | --- |
| Perceived energy | low, light, moderate, high |
| Mental clarity | clean, focused, diffuse, tense |
| Onset of the effect | quick, gradual, slow |
| Perceived duration of the effect | short, medium, long |
| Comfort | comfortable, neutral, excessive |

Dose, temperature, hydration, individual tolerance, and context strongly alter this axis.

## 10. Operational Summary

A good yerba mate analysis does not ask only "what does this remind me of?" It asks:

1. is this typical of the style or coherent with the intended profile?
2. is it clean?
3. is it integrated?
4. does it evolve well throughout the session?

In parallel, it is worth recording how intense and persistent the sample is and, when relevant, how the perceived physiological effect manifests.

When these four answers are handled rigorously, the vocabulary becomes more useful, judgment becomes more stable, and the sensory wheel starts representing the product much more faithfully.
